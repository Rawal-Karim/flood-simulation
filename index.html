NAI Watershed Flood Simulation

Sindh Irrigation Department · Cluster 4 · Right Bank Indus

🎯 What Is This Simulation?

Every monsoon season, heavy rains fall on the Kirthar and Koh-e-Sulaiman mountain ranges to the west of the Indus River in Sindh, Pakistan. Because the mountains are rocky and steep, rainwater cannot soak into the ground — it rushes down dry riverbeds called Nais (hill torrents) at enormous speed.

These Nais carry millions of gallons of muddy floodwater across the flat Kohistan plains, filling lakes, straining flood barriers, and threatening villages and farmland before eventually draining into the Indus River.

This simulation recreates that journey in 3D — showing how a monsoon storm high in the mountains turns into a flood emergency on the plains below, and how the Sindh Irrigation Department's infrastructure responds to contain it.

📅 The Five Phases

1 — Dry Season

Before the monsoon. All Nais are empty dry riverbeds. Lakes are at low levels. The Indus flows normally at around 95,000 cusecs.

2 — Rain Begins

Monsoon clouds arrive. Rainfall intensifies over the mountains. The Indus starts rising. You can see and hear the rain building on screen.

3 — Surge

This is the critical phase. One by one the Nais activate and surge with floodwater (shown as muddy brown sheets spreading across the terrain). Lakes begin filling rapidly. The Flood Protection Bund comes under pressure.

4 — Peak Flood

All Nais are flowing. Lakes are near capacity. The Indus is carrying over 500,000–635,000 cusecs — more than 6× its normal flow. This matches real data recorded in August–September 2025.

5 — Recession

Floodwaters slowly drain. Nais go dry again. Lakes gradually return to normal. The cycle resets and repeats.

📊 Understanding the Chart

The chart at the bottom shows Indus River discharge — the volume of water passing through the river per second, measured in cusecs (cubic feet per second).

Normal Flow

Below 200,000 cusecs. Safe. No flood risk.

Low Flood

200,000 – 350,000 cusecs. River is high. Monitoring active.

High Flood

500,000 – 700,000 cusecs. Serious risk. Barrages under stress.

Super Flood

Above 900,000 cusecs. Catastrophic. Rare but devastating.

The two orange/red spike labels on the chart are real recorded peaks from the 2025 monsoon season: 543,184 cusecs on 24 August and 635,759 cusecs on 14 September.

📈 Understanding the Metrics

Active Nais

How many hill torrents are currently carrying floodwater. There are 9 confirmed Nais modelled — Nai Naig, Nai Bhan, Nai Pakho, Nai Dugdu, Nai Khedro, Nai Gaj, Nai Ranjo, Nai Chandio, and Nai Baran. When 3 or more activate simultaneously a flash flood alert is triggered.

FP Bund Status

The Flood Protection Bund is a 234 km earthen embankment running along the right bank of the Indus. It is the last line of defence between the floodwater and populated areas. "Under Pressure" means the bund is holding back an active surge.

Lake Fill Levels — Manchar, Hamal, Gaj Dam, Darawai

Manchar Lake is the largest natural freshwater lake in Pakistan. Hamal and Darawai are depression lakes. Gaj Dam is a small storage structure. All four receive runoff from the Nais. Their fill % shows how much of their capacity is currently occupied by floodwater.

Kotri Barrage (River Tip)

Kotri Barrage is the southernmost barrage on the Indus. The live reading shows the flow rate passing through it in cusecs — the downstream gateway before the river reaches the Arabian Sea.

🗺 The Map Layers

Blue dashed lines — Hill Torrents (Nais)

The dry riverbeds of 9 confirmed Nais flowing from the Kirthar Mountains. They turn into rushing floodwater sheets during the surge phase.

Green line — FP Bund

The 234 km earthen flood protection embankment. Glows orange when under pressure from active surges.

Teal lines — Thatta Division Canals

The irrigation canal network downstream — K.B. Feeder Upper & Lower, Link Canal, Jam Branch, and 17 minor distributaries — mapped from the Sindh Irrigation Department's 2025 network survey. Visible when you pan south.

Yellow diamonds — Barrages

Guddu, Sukkur, and Kotri Barrages are concrete structures that regulate Indus flow, divert water into canals, and provide flood data. They glow when the river is in flood stage.
